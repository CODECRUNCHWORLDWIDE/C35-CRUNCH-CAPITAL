# Challenge 1 — Covenant Breach Simulation

**Time:** ~90 minutes. **Difficulty:** Medium-high. **No single right implementation.**

## The scenario

Crunch Machine Co.'s board approved the $20,000,000 term loan from Lecture 2 — 5-year, 7.5%, secured, with a **maximum leverage covenant of 3.00× Debt/EBITDA** and a **minimum interest coverage covenant of 4.00× EBIT/Interest**. Six months later, a slowdown in industrial demand is hitting the whole sector, and the CFO wants to know, before it happens: **exactly how far can EBITDA fall before this loan trips a covenant — and which covenant breaks first?** This isn't an academic question — a breached covenant typically lets the lender demand immediate repayment or force a renegotiation at worse terms, at exactly the moment the company can least afford either.

## What you're building

A small simulation engine that takes an EBITDA decline percentage and reports whether either covenant is breached, then sweeps across a range of declines to find the exact breaking point for each.

## Your task

### Part 1 — The covenant test functions

Write two functions:

```python
def leverage_ratio(total_debt: float, ebitda: float) -> float:
    """Debt / EBITDA."""
    ...

def interest_coverage_ratio(ebit: float, total_interest: float) -> float:
    """EBIT / total interest expense."""
    ...
```

Confirm both reproduce Lecture 2, Section 6's baseline numbers: `leverage_ratio(50_000_000, 22_000_000)` ≈ `2.273`, `interest_coverage_ratio(18_000_000, 3_300_000)` ≈ `5.455`.

### Part 2 — Sweep EBITDA decline scenarios

Write a function `covenant_check(ebitda_decline_pct: float) -> dict` that:

1. Applies the decline to the baseline EBITDA ($22,000,000): `ebitda = 22_000_000 * (1 - ebitda_decline_pct)`.
2. Holds **depreciation & amortization constant** at $4,000,000 (a recession cuts revenue and margin, not the depreciation schedule on assets the company already owns), so `ebit = ebitda - 4_000_000`.
3. Holds **total debt and total interest constant** at this week's baseline (existing $30M + new $20M; year-1 total interest $3,300,000) — the point of this challenge is isolating the effect of an EBITDA shock, not modeling a simultaneous refinancing.
4. Computes both ratios and returns whether each covenant is breached (`leverage > 3.0`, `coverage < 4.0`).

Run it for declines of `0%, 5%, 10%, 15%, 20%, 25%, 30%, 35%` and print a table: decline %, EBITDA, EBIT, leverage ratio, leverage breach (Y/N), coverage ratio, coverage breach (Y/N).

### Part 3 — Find the exact breakeven

For **each** covenant separately, solve algebraically for the exact EBITDA decline percentage that puts the ratio exactly at its limit (leverage = 3.00×, coverage = 4.00×). Report both breakeven percentages. **Which covenant breaks first** as EBITDA declines — leverage or coverage — and by how many percentage points of decline does the first one lead the second?

### Part 4 — Store the sweep in SQL and query it

Load your Part 2 sweep results into a table `covenant_stress_test` (columns: `decline_pct`, `ebitda`, `ebit`, `leverage_ratio`, `leverage_breach`, `coverage_ratio`, `coverage_breach`). Then answer with **SQL**, not Python:

1. What is the smallest `decline_pct` in your table where **either** covenant shows a breach?
2. At the 30% decline scenario, by how much does the coverage ratio fall short of its 4.00× minimum, in absolute ratio terms (e.g., "0.55× short")?

### Part 5 — Write the recommendation

In `writeup.md`, answer in 300–400 words:

1. Which covenant is the **binding constraint** — the one that breaks first as things get worse — and does that surprise you given the two covenants' baseline headroom (2.273× vs. a 3.00× limit, and 5.455× vs. a 4.00× minimum)?
2. Name one lever, besides "EBITDA recovers," that the company could realistically pull to cure a covenant breach *without* refinancing the whole loan (hint: think about what's on both sides of each ratio — can either side move independently of EBITDA?).
3. Should the CFO have negotiated tighter or looser covenant thresholds on this loan, given how close a plausible-but-not-extreme recession scenario (say, a 20–25% EBITDA decline — not a rare event for an industrial manufacturer) comes to breaching them? Defend your answer with the numbers from Part 3.

## Constraints

- Your `covenant_check` function must work for **any** decline percentage, not just the eight in Part 2's table — prove it by running one arbitrary value (e.g., 17.5%) and confirming the output is internally consistent (EBIT, both ratios, both breach flags all agree with each other).
- No spreadsheet, per this course's data tooling rule — the sweep lives in a table, generated by code.
- Show your Part 3 algebra, not just the final breakeven numbers — a correct answer without the derivation doesn't prove you understand *why* it's the answer.

## Hints

<details>
<summary>On solving for the breakeven algebraically</summary>

For leverage: `total_debt / ebitda = 3.0` → `ebitda = total_debt / 3.0`. Compare that EBITDA to the $22,000,000 baseline to get the decline percentage. For coverage: `ebit / total_interest = 4.0` → `ebit = 4.0 × total_interest` → since `ebit = ebitda - 4,000,000`, solve for the `ebitda` that produces that `ebit`, then compare to baseline the same way.

</details>

<details>
<summary>On which covenant breaks first not matching intuition</summary>

Baseline headroom (how far each ratio sits from its limit) is not the same as how *sensitive* each ratio is to an EBITDA decline — leverage's denominator is EBITDA directly, while coverage's numerator (EBIT) falls by the *same dollar amount* as EBITDA (since D&A is held constant), which is a *larger percentage move* on EBIT's smaller base. That's the mechanic worth explaining in your writeup, not just observing.

</details>
