**Q13.** Which of the following is a cost of **debt** financing that equity financing does **not** carry, according to Lecture 3?

**Q14.** A CFO recommends debt over equity for a financing need, citing only that debt's after-tax cost (5.625%) is lower than equity's cost (11%). According to Lecture 3, Section 8, what is missing from that recommendation?

- A) Nothing — cost is the only axis that matters in a financing decision.
- B) The recommendation should also address capacity (is there room under existing covenants?), control (how much ownership/voting is at stake?), and flexibility/signaling (can the company reliably meet a fixed obligation, and how will the market read either choice?).
- C) The CFO should have used the coupon rate instead of the after-tax cost.
- D) Debt is never actually cheaper than equity, so the recommendation is simply wrong.

## Answer key

<details>
<summary>Reveal after attempting</summary>

1. **B** — the new-issue discount incentivizes investors to buy the new block now instead of waiting to buy on the open market, which is why follow-on offerings are almost never priced at the exact current market price.
2. **B** — since the fee is a percentage of gross proceeds, and the company needs a specific *net* amount after that fee is taken out, gross proceeds must be net proceeds divided by (1 − fee percentage), not multiplied or subtracted directly.
3. **B** — fractional shares can't be sold; rounding down or to the nearest share risks the company netting less than its funding target, so the convention is always to round up.
4. **B** — the mechanism of dilution: an unchanged share count divided by a growing total produces a shrinking percentage, with no shares ever changing hands from the existing holder.
5. **B** — percentage ownership and dollar value move independently; a smaller slice of a sufficiently bigger, more valuable company (because the raise funded something that created real value) can still be worth more dollars than a larger slice of the smaller company before the raise.
6. **B** — interest is always computed on the current outstanding balance, which starts at its maximum in period 1 and shrinks every period after as principal gets paid down, so no other period's interest charge is ever larger than the first's.
7. **C** — `7.5% × (1 − 0.25) = 5.625%`, the after-tax cost of debt formula from Lecture 3.
8. **B** — interest expense reduces a company's taxable income (the tax shield), but dividends and other equity returns are paid out of after-tax profit and are not tax-deductible, so equity financing has no equivalent shield.
9. **D** — `50,000,000 / 22,000,000 ≈ 3.27×`, which is **above** (outside) a 3.00× maximum-leverage covenant — a breach.
10. **B** — a higher interest coverage ratio means operating earnings (EBIT) exceed interest obligations by a wider margin, i.e., more cushion to service the debt.
11. **A** — seniority and collateral are real, structural differences in risk to the lender; a secured, senior claim is safer because it gets repaid first and has a specific asset backing it, and safer lending commands a lower required return.
12. **B** — pecking order theory predicts a negative or skeptical market reaction to an equity announcement, on the logic that management would likely prefer cheaper financing (debt, or internal cash) if they were confident the stock was fairly or under-valued.
13. **A** — debt's fixed, mandatory payment obligation (due regardless of business performance) has no equity equivalent; equity's obligations (dividends) are discretionary. The other three options (B, C, D) are actually costs associated with *equity*, not debt, making them incorrect as "a cost debt has that equity doesn't."
14. **B** — Lecture 3's framework explicitly requires weighing capacity, control, and flexibility/signaling alongside cost; a recommendation based on cost alone, even when cost genuinely favors one option, is incomplete.

</details>
