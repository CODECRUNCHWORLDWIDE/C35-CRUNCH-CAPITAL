# Week 11 — Challenges

Two open-ended problems. Unlike the exercises, these ask you to extend the model past what the lectures walked through — the skill is judgment and careful multi-step arithmetic, not recall. Do them after the three exercises.

Both challenges continue from **Lecture 2's canonical post-Series-A cap table** (16,615,500 fully diluted shares — the clean lecture numbers, not the Exercise 3 variant that includes Sam Okafor's advisor grant). Starting fresh from the lecture keeps the numbers you're checking your work against unambiguous. If you'd rather continue from your own Exercise 3 build, that's a valid — and arguably more realistic — choice; just say so explicitly in your write-up and expect every downstream number to shift slightly.

1. **[Challenge 1 — Multi-round dilution](challenge-01-multi-round-dilution.md)** — add a Series B and track every holder's ownership percentage across three rounds: Founding → Series A → Series B. *(~60 min.)*
2. **[Challenge 2 — Waterfall under preferences](challenge-02-waterfall-under-preferences.md)** — extend the exit waterfall to three financing rounds, and compare non-participating vs. participating preferred at the same exit price. *(~60 min.)*

## How these are judged

There's no single-number answer key for the open-ended parts. Instead, each challenge tells you what a *strong* submission looks like. You're being graded on:

- **Correctness of the mechanics** — does your dilution or waterfall math follow the rules from the lectures, applied consistently?
- **Explicit assumptions** — when a term (pool size, preference stack order, participation) isn't fully specified, did you state the interpretation you chose?
- **Reconciliation** — do your percentages sum to 100%? Does your waterfall's total payout equal the exit price exactly (or within a few dollars of rounding)?

Keep your work in `challenge-01.md` / `challenge-02.md` with your queries **and** your written reasoning. The reasoning is the point.
